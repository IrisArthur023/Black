Entry #14,802 / Unsolved

The Big Bang's
Missing Lithium.

Q

Query-LAB Editorial Team

Updated March 2026 • 12 min read

"If our models of the Big Bang are correct, the universe is missing two-thirds of its Lithium. This isn't just a rounding error; it's a foundational crisis in physics."

The Discrepancy

Standard Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) predicts that the universe's earliest phase should have produced a specific amount of Lithium-7. However, when we point our telescopes at the oldest, most metal-poor stars in our galaxy (the Spite Plateau), we observe only about 30% of that predicted amount.

The 8-Year Deadlock

Since 2018, the discrepancy has only deepened. While we’ve solved similar problems for Deuterium and Helium, Lithium remains the lone outlier. Online communities and research forums have posed three primary solutions that remain unproven:

  • Stellar Depletion: Perhaps stars "eat" their own lithium over time. However, this doesn't explain why stars of varying temperatures show the exact same depletion levels.
  • New Physics: Proposed theories suggest decaying dark matter or varying fundamental constants during the first minutes of the universe could have suppressed lithium production.
  • The Beryllium Path: Most Lithium-7 actually originates as Beryllium-7. If something interfered with Beryllium creation, the Lithium would never form.

Why It Matters

Solving the Lithium Problem isn't just about one element; it's about verifying if our understanding of the first three minutes of time is accurate. Until this ledger is balanced, our story of the origin of everything remains incomplete.
